Foundation

What Is a Personal Learning Network?

A PLN is a network of people, resources, and tools that supports ongoing professional learning through social media and digital platforms.

Rajagopal et al. (2012) define a PLN as a system where someone "intentionally builds, maintains and activates contacts within her personal network for the purpose of improving her learning." The key word is intentionally. A PLN is not the list of accounts you follow. It is the deliberate curation of connections that help you grow professionally.

The theoretical foundation is connectivism, which argues that learning in the digital age happens through networks rather than through individual knowledge acquisition. Your ability to find, evaluate, and connect information across sources is more valuable than memorizing any single body of knowledge (Siemens, 2005).

A PLN differs from a social network in purpose. A social network connects you to people you know. A PLN connects you to people you learn from. The overlap exists, but the intention is different. Every person you follow, every community you join, and every platform you use is a design decision about what your learning environment looks like.

Throughout this guide, we will explore how to build a PLN that is diverse, media-literate, privacy-conscious, and professionally effective, drawing on the frameworks and research covered in EDCI 338.

Digital Identity

Managing Your Online Presence

Your digital identity is everything you post, share, how you engage, and how you treat other people online (Stoller, 2016). It is your footprint on the web.

Controlled presence is content you intentionally create and publish. These are the building blocks of your professional digital identity:

Professional profiles
Published content
Code contributions
Portfolio work
Conference talks
Blog posts

Uncontrolled presence exists without your direct input. danah boyd (2012) argues that privacy is no longer an individual property because others' decisions about your data affect your identity:

Tagged photos
Client reviews
Forum mentions
Cached profiles
Tracking data
Search results

Spracklen (2015) frames social media as a leisure space where identity is actively constructed. Managing the convergence of personal and professional identities requires deliberate platform strategy:

Treat platforms differently. LinkedIn for industry content. GitHub for code. Twitter for personality and ideas. Instagram for personal. Each serves a distinct identity function.
Audit regularly. Your digital identity is doing a job interview 24/7. The question is not whether to have a presence, but whether the one you have is working for you.

Mapping Your Digital Practices

White and Le Cornu (2017) propose a more useful model than "digital native/immigrant." Instead, people are Visitors (task-based, no trace) or Residents (identity-building, persistent presence) across different platforms.

The V&R framework reveals patterns that platform lists cannot. A professional who is a Resident on GitHub (contributing code daily, building reputation through commits) but a Visitor on YouTube (watching tutorials, leaving no trace) uses the same internet in fundamentally different ways across those two platforms. Mapping these modes of engagement helps identify where your PLN is strong and where it has gaps.

Inclusion & Diversity

Building an Inclusive PLN

Dr. Shelley Moore (2021) argues that inclusion is not assimilation. It is finding balance between communities of identity and communities of diversity.

The most valuable PLNs are not echo chambers of agreement. They are networks where different perspectives collide constructively. Obama's Stanford address (2022) warned that social media platforms can fragment discourse into "silos of affirmation" unless users deliberately seek diverse voices.

Clark and Aufderheide (2009) call for media platforms that are "open, dynamic, and participatory." The same principle applies to your PLN: it should amplify views you have not encountered, not just validate the ones you already hold.

Media Literacy

Evaluating Information in Your PLN

Media literacy is the ability to critically analyze, evaluate, and create media (Trilling & Fadel, 2009). In a PLN context, it determines whether your network makes you smarter or just more confident.

Hirst (2019) argues that fake news is not primarily an information problem. It is a business model. Content designed to provoke outrage generates clicks, which generates revenue. Understanding that supply chain is more useful than fact-checking individual articles. The real question is not "is this true?" but "why does this exist, and who profits from me sharing it?"

Sophie Lui, a Global BC news anchor, described social media as a place that "can eat away at my soul" when she follows accounts out of obligation rather than value. A media-literate PLN is one where you follow sources because they make you think, not because the algorithm put them in front of you.

How Media Literacy Changes Your Response
Without Media Literacy

See a shocking headline about a Canadian politician.

Feel outraged. Share immediately.

Read the comments. Get angrier.

Move on. Never check if it was true.

The misinformation has now reached your entire network.

→
With Media Literacy

See a shocking headline. Pause. Notice the emotional trigger.

Check: who published this? When was the domain registered?

Lateral read: what do other sources say about this claim?

Check the subject's official channels for confirmation or denial.

If unverified, do not share. The chain of misinformation stops with you.

Privacy & Data

The Cost of Connection

Every PLN platform is a data relationship. In Canada, PIPEDA governs how your personal information is collected, used, and disclosed.

Live Data Flow Visualization

PIPEDA requires organizations to get consent before collecting personal information, collect only what is necessary, and allow users to access and correct their data. But in practice, the patchwork of federal (PIPEDA), provincial (BC's FIPPA, Alberta's PIPA), and sector-specific regulations means your data is subject to different rules depending on where the platform is hosted and who is accessing it.

The DAIS report on generative AI and youth privacy (Kwok & Tessono, 2025) highlights that existing privacy frameworks were not designed for AI systems that build predictive profiles from behavioral data at scale.

The tension between privacy and PLN effectiveness is real. You cannot build a professional network while remaining invisible. The willingness to be searchable, attributable, and publicly engaged is what makes a PLN functional. The question is not whether to accept this trade-off, but whether you are making it with full awareness of what you are giving up.

When your institution directs you to use a specific platform, ask: where is the data hosted? Who has access? What happens to it after the course ends? These are not paranoid questions. They are responsible ones.

AI & Social Media

AI in Your PLN

AI is reshaping how content is created, distributed, and consumed in every PLN. The question is whether you are using it critically or being used by it.

Estaiteyeh and Mindzak (2025) found that Canadian teacher candidates were generally inclined to use AI tools themselves but expressed negative views toward their students using the same tools. This inconsistency reveals a tension that exists across every profession: we adopt tools before we understand them, and we hold others to standards we do not apply to ourselves.

MediaSmarts warns that AI-generated content has grown faster than the public's ability to critically assess it. The gap between production capacity and literacy is the core challenge for digital citizenship. "Doomscrolling now includes AI slop," as the course instructor put it. A responsible PLN requires the ability to distinguish between human insight and synthetic content.

Measuring Success

Is Your PLN Working?

Qualman (2012) argues that digital leadership is about creating value for others, not building the biggest following. The metrics that matter are about learning, not likes.

Hermida (2014) argues that sharing is a fundamental human behavior that social media amplified, not created. We share to process our own thinking, to help others, and to strengthen social bonds. The best PLN is one where sharing is the learning, not just a channel for distributing what you already know.

A PLN is successful when it consistently exposes you to ideas you would not have found on your own, connects you to people who challenge your thinking, and gives you a platform to contribute back to your professional community.

PLN Health Check
I learned something new from my network this week

If the answer is no, your PLN has stagnated. Add a new source in a field you know nothing about.

Qualman (2012): digital leadership creates value through learning
I contributed something useful to my network this week

Sharing is the learning. Write a post, reply to a thread, or share a resource with commentary. Consumption without contribution is passive.

Hermida (2014): we share to process our own thinking
Someone in my network made me reconsider an assumption

This is the highest signal of a healthy PLN. If nobody is challenging your thinking, you are in an echo chamber.

Clark & Aufderheide (2009): "open, dynamic, and participatory"
I can name three people I follow for different perspectives

Not just different topics. Different worldviews. If you cannot name them, your diversity is incidental, not intentional.

Moore (2021): balance identity communities with diversity communities
My platform choices are deliberate, not habitual

Map your V&R profile. If you cannot explain why you use a platform, you are using it out of habit, not strategy.

White & Le Cornu (2017): Visitor vs Resident modes of engagement
I can explain why I use each platform in my PLN

Every platform serves a function. LinkedIn for professional reach. GitHub for code reputation. Twitter for real-time discourse. If a platform has no function, cut it.

Technical Stack & Implementation

This project was built as a single-page application using modern web technologies and open-source libraries. Every interactive element, animation, and data visualization runs client-side in the browser with no server backend required. The entire application is contained in a single HTML file deployed via GitHub Pages.

Data Visualization
D3.js v7
Force-directed graph simulation with Verlet integration physics engine. Implements Barnes-Hut approximation for n-body charge forces, collision detection via quadtree spatial indexing, and configurable link distance springs. Supports real-time node/link CRUD operations with dynamic simulation reheating.
Charts
Chart.js v4
Canvas-based radar chart with responsive resizing, animated dataset transitions, and configurable polar coordinate scales. Platform comparison data is dynamically swapped on user interaction with smooth interpolation between states.
Animation Engine
GSAP 3.12 + ScrollTrigger
High-performance animation runtime using requestAnimationFrame scheduling. ScrollTrigger plugin monitors viewport intersection for scroll-activated counter animations with configurable easing curves and snap-to-integer rounding.
Scroll Animations
AOS (Animate on Scroll)
Intersection Observer-based library triggering CSS animations as elements enter the viewport. Configured with customizable offsets, durations, and per-element delay cascading for staggered reveal sequences across card grids.
Styling
Custom CSS with Design Tokens
CSS custom properties (design tokens) for theming with automatic dark mode via prefers-color-scheme media query. Responsive grid layouts using CSS Grid and Flexbox. Cubic-bezier transitions, gradient animations with background-size keyframes, and backdrop-filter blur effects.
Deployment
GitHub Pages (Static CDN)
Zero-config static deployment via Git push. All dependencies loaded from CDN (jsDelivr, unpkg) for optimal caching and global edge distribution. No build pipeline, no server runtime, no database. Single HTML file architecture for maximum portability.
