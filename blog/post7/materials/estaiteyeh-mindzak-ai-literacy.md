Abstract 
Preparing new teachers for the reality of artificial intelligence in education (AIEd) 
has become a pressing issue. This study was conducted in a Canadian teacher 
education program that offers a course on digital technologies incorporating a 
module on AIEd. This paper addresses two research questions: 1) What were 
teacher candidates’ (TCs’) experiences with the module on AIEd? and 2) What 
were TCs’ views on the use of AI by themselves and their students? The study 
employed an explanatory mixed methods design, combining quantitative and 
qualitative data gathered via a survey administered to TCs directly following their 
module completion. Participants were two cohorts of TCs (108 TCs in 2024 and 
104 TCs in 2025). Findings show TCs’ satisfaction with the module as they 
highlighted three major benefits: offering useful teaching resources; more 
acceptance to explore the technology and embrace it critically; and promoting AI 
literacy. TCs expressed an inclination to use AI as teachers. However, they 
expressed negative views toward their students’ use of AI. Additionally, most TCs 
demonstrated developing levels of critical AI literacy, especially among the most 
recent cohort. This research offers insights into promoting TCs’ AI literacy and 
presents implications for teacher education research, practice, and policy.  
 
   
Introduction 
 
The rapid rise of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI), notably tools like ChatGPT, is 
reshaping education systems worldwide. For students, GenAI offers opportunities to personalize 
learning, improve writing, and access on-demand support, yet also risks promoting surface-level 
thinking and undermining academic ethics and integrity. For new teachers, GenAI presents both 
promise and pressure. While it can streamline lesson planning, assessment design, and 
differentiation, it simultaneously demands new forms of pedagogical literacy and digital fluency

(Chiu et al., 2022; Ng et al. 2021). In short, GenAI is significantly impacting both teaching as well 
as teachers themselves. 
As teacher candidates (TCs) transition into professional classroom roles, they are stepping 
into a profession already undergoing rapid technological transformation. Having recently 
experienced GenAI as students themselves, they now face the added complexity of modeling its 
responsible use, designing GenAI-aware instruction, and responding to shifting student behaviors 
around academic integrity. This dual identity—both as learners and emerging professionals—
creates unique tensions. TCs today must quickly develop an understanding of AI not only to design 
relevant and engaging learning experiences, but also to navigate student misuse, evolving 
assessment norms, and institutional policies still struggling to keep pace with technological 
change. The result is a transition into teaching that blends traditional pedagogical responsibilities 
with new digital demands (Bae et al., 2024). 
Such concerns remain salient in places including Ontario, Canada, where GenAI usage in 
schools and classrooms is becoming increasingly ubiquitous. According to Janzen et al. (2024), in 
their survey of 881 Canadian students, 77% of respondents reported using tools like ChatGPT, and 
among those, 72% had used them specifically for coursework. This sharp rise from the previous 
year is part of a broader trend in educational technology adoption. For TCs—many of whom were 
recent users themselves—this widespread adoption creates immediate challenges as they shift from 
GenAI as a personal study aid to managing its classroom application and implications--where 
issues of ethics, originality, and pedagogy come to the fore. 
Similarly, Chow (2023) reveals an emergent digital divide within Ontario’s educational 
workforce regarding GenAI. While 6% of educators report daily use and 7% identify as “experts,” 
27% have never used these tools, often citing a lack of training and resources. This disparity 
highlights a notable gap—not only in access, but in pedagogical readiness. The report also notes 
that 76% of Ontario educators would attend a short GenAI training if offered, signalling strong 
interest but insufficient institutional support. To address this, Chow (2023) calls for comprehensive 
AI policies, targeted professional development, and ethical frameworks to equip both educators 
and students with the skills necessary in an AI-driven world. Key competencies such as prompt 
engineering, ethical reasoning, and critical thinking should become central to teacher preparation. 
Teacher education programs, therefore, appear to face the challenge of ensuring that novice 
educators are not only competent users of GenAI, but also capable of guiding their own future 
students toward responsible, equitable, and pedagogically sound uses of technology. 
 
Research rationale and objectives 
 
While considerable attention has been paid to how students engage with GenAI tools, there is a 
growing need to understand how TCs are navigating GenAI as students-teachers themselves. As 
students in the teacher education program, these candidates face the dual challenge of learning 
about GenAI tools and effectively integrating those into their pedagogical practice as teachers– if 
they choose to. TCs must simultaneously adapt to professional responsibilities in a context marked 
by new changes and technological uncertainties. This dual positioning creates a complex and 
emergent dynamic that has implications for teacher development, identity, assessment practices, 
classroom management and beyond as new teachers embark on their careers.  
Overall, there remains a gap in preparing TCs for AI, particularly in supporting their 
development as both learners of AI literacy and future teachers of it (Chiu et al., 2025; Guan et al., 
2025; Kohnke et al., 2023), and hence this research study. This study was conducted within a 
teacher education program at a university in Ontario, Canada which offers a stand-alone course on

Literature Review 
 
Uses of AI in education  
 
The uses of GenAI in education span an increasingly wide range of applications, which can be 
broadly grouped into four categories: administrative tasks, instruction, learning, and assessment. 
Firstly, AI appears effective in automating various administrative tasks, including enrollment, 
student data management, grading, course scheduling, and financial logistics. Such automation can 
help to reduce the administrative burden on educators and administrators, allowing them to allocate 
more time to teaching and learning activities (Adeshola & Adepoju, 2023; Fullan et al., 2024; 
Holmes, 2020). Nevertheless, over-reliance on AI automation may diminish human oversight and 
lead to more opaque decision-making processes (Selwyn, 2021; Williamson & Eynon, 2020). AI 
automation thus still faces the challenge of maintaining accountability mechanisms, ethical 
guidelines, and human involvement. 
With respect to instructional applications, GenAI offers teachers the ability to create 
personalized and adaptive learning experiences (Grassini, 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). GenAI can also 
have a relevant impact on student learning by enabling greater personalization and customization 
of curricula and content. Such tools can adjust learning materials to match the unique needs and 
capabilities of individual learners, promoting a more tailored educational experience (L. Chen et 
al., 2020). Additionally, AI-powered platforms can perform teacher-like functions, such as lesson 
planning and assessment, further enhancing instructional support (L. Chen et al., 2020; Trust et 
al., 2024).  
Additionally, AI-powered chatbots and virtual assistants are always available providing 
students with immediate responses to questions and guidance on navigating learning resources 
regardless of location (Rahman & Watanobe, 2023; Trust et al., 2023). For students with 
disabilities, AI aids accessibility by providing adaptive learning materials and tools, including 
speech-to-text and text-to-speech features (Chopra et al., 2024; Shuford, 2023). 
Nevertheless, overreliance on algorithm-driven content (also known as offloading) may 
limit teacher decision-making abilities and autonomy and may reduce opportunities for 
spontaneous, context-specific pedagogy (Selwyn, 2021). There is also the risk that AI-based 
personalization, while efficient, may narrow students’ exposure to diverse perspectives or

of digital infrastructure in under-resourced schools may also exacerbate educational inequalities. 
Thus, while GenAI can enrich instruction, its integration must be thoughtfully managed alongside

In the area of assessment, GenAI facilitates various advancements that revise the evaluation 
process, such as automated grading and feedback systems (Cope et al., 2021; Trust et al., 2023). 
Real-time assessments enable educators to evaluate skills and knowledge immediately as students 
engage with tasks (X. Chen et al., 2022). In addition, learning analytics, involving the collection 
and analysis of data about students’ learning activities, provides data-driven insights that may 
optimize educational outcomes (Mhlanga, 2023; Trust et al., 2023). However, GenAI systems still 
struggle to accurately assess creativity, critical thinking, or culturally diverse forms of expression, 
especially in open-ended tasks. Furthermore, overreliance on data-driven approaches can lead to a 
reductionist view of learning, privileging what is measurable over what is meaningful and 
introduces problematic ethical dilemmas around student surveillance, data privacy, and consent. 
(Williamson & Eynon, 2020).  
 
Challenges and concerns  
 
The rise of GenAI in education has then raised several challenges and concerns that must be 
carefully addressed. One of the most prominent issues is academic integrity. AI tools, particularly 
GenAI such as ChatGPT, have been associated with an increase in cheating and plagiarism, 
prompting some schools to ban such tools altogether (Farrokhnia et al., 2023; Grassini, 2023; 
Kumar et al., 2023; Lo, 2023). Another significant concern is AI's potential impact on learning, 
particularly in the development of critical thinking and problem-solving skills. With AI systems 
providing quick solutions to queries, students may be less likely to engage in deeper cognitive 
processes necessary for meaningful learning. This has led educators to question whether AI is 
genuinely supporting student learning or merely offering shortcuts that undermine educational 
goals (Farrokhnia et al., 2023; Lo, 2023; Trust et al., 2024).  
Additionally, GenAI has also been scrutinized for issues related to bias and fairness, as it 
may perpetuate or even exacerbate existing societal biases, resulting in unfair or discriminatory 
outcomes for certain groups. This is especially problematic in areas such as assessment, 
personalized learning, and risk identification, where AI can unintentionally reinforce inequality 
(Akgun & Greenhow, 2022; OECD, 2023; Yan et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2021). Privacy and data 
security issues are also critical as the sensitive nature of student and teacher data collected by AI 
systems raises both ethical and legal questions (Akgun & Greenhow, 2022; UNESCO, 2019; Yan 
et al., 2023). Transparency and explainability have also been noted as problematic, as there is no 
clear understanding of how AI models function, including details on data sources, decision-making 
algorithms, and how outcomes like recommendations or assessments are generated. Understanding 
such decision-making processes remains crucial for fair implementation and to foster trust in AI-
driven educational tools (Radwan & McGinty, 2024; UNESCO, 2024; Yan et al., 2023). Finally, 
potential equity issues arise with AI implementation, as disparities in access may widen the digital 
divide (Sanusi et al., 2024; Vesna et al., 2025).

GenAI in teacher education  
 
The emergent scholarly literature on teachers’ and TCs’ views on AI in education reflects a blend 
of optimism and concern, noting the complex attitudes surrounding this transformative technology 
(Çayak, 2024; Nyaaba et al., 2024; Viberg et al., 2023). Teachers often see AI as both a promising 
tool and a potential threat to learning (Trust et al., 2024). Research indicates that while many 
teachers have overall positive attitudes toward AI (Altinay et al., 2024; Zawacki-Richter et al., 
2019), they also voice apprehensions about the challenges it poses, particularly regarding ethical 
and logistical considerations (Altinay et al., 2024). New teachers show varied levels of both 
openness and anxiety toward AI (Hopcan et al., 2024). Remarkably, teachers from technology-
related fields tend to hold more favourable views on AI compared to their peers in other disciplines 
(Hopcan et al., 2024; Ng et al., 2021). A common concern among teachers is that AI might replace 
human educators, thereby jeopardizing jobs and possibly diminishing educational quality (Hopcan 
et al., 2024; Sanusi et al., 2024).  
Newer teachers often tend to remain more optimistic than in-service teachers, viewing AI 
more positively (Altinay et al., 2024). TCs understand that AI is here to stay and can offer 
significant advantages to education. They believe that AI proficiency is becoming a key indicator 
of teacher quality and an advantage in the profession. However, TCs highlight a critical need for 
more comprehensive training and resources to develop their AI literacy, emphasizing the 
importance of continuous learning and keeping up with technological advancements (Kohnke et 
al., 2023). TCs require substantial support in developing their AI literacy, including technical 
skills, ethical understanding, and pedagogical strategies for truly collaborative AI integration (Hur, 
2025). They desire workshops, courses on AI skills, and clear guidance on how to approach AI 
correctly, integrate it into their curriculum design, and guide students in using it responsibly (Guan 
et al., 2025; Kohnke et al., 2023).  
Promoting TCs’ AI literacy involves a multifaceted approach that addresses their initial 
lack of knowledge, builds confidence, and tackles their concerns through structured interventions 
and continuous support. Hur (2025) recommends integrating AI literacy lessons into existing 
technology integration courses, particularly through hands-on learning experiences. These 
experiences must go beyond specific tools to include facilitating class discussions where TCs share 
their thoughts and concerns about using AI, with a focus on its ethical and responsible use (Hur, 
2005; Kohnke, 2023). Training should also equip TCs with the knowledge and skills to critically 
evaluate AI-generated content, rather than blindly trusting its authenticity or accuracy (Chiu et al, 
2025). Teacher preparation must promote the understanding that AI proficiency is an evolving 
competency that requires continuous professional development (Kohnke et al., 2023) and that AI 
can support teachers rather than replace them (Hur, 2025). 
All the above reiterates that AI literacy must become a fundamental component in teacher 
education programs (Estaiteyeh & McQuirter, 2024), warranting this research study on how 
teacher educators can prepare TCs in AIEd. This study presents an example of structured 
professional development on AIEd incorporated within a digital technology course in pre-service 
teacher education. The study explores how TCs perceive such training and how TCs’ view GenAI 
as both learners and future teachers.

Theoretical Framework   
 
AI literacy 
 
The evolution of digital literacy serves as a foundational precursor to the emerging concept 
of “AI literacy”, particularly within educational contexts (Dagostino et al., 2024). Digital literacy, 
broadly defined, encompasses the knowledge and skills necessary to effectively navigate, evaluate 
and engage with digital technologies. Initially, digital literacy focused on core competencies—
basic computer operation, information retrieval, media analysis, and subsequent understandings of 
working and living in an online, connected world. Over time, digital literacy has expanded to 
include a deeper, even critical, understanding of both digital content and citizenship (Belshaw, 
2012). As technology has shifted from static tools to more dynamic systems, the scope of digital 
literacy appears to expand to encompass the notion of AI literacy.  Hence, GenAI likely represents 
the next layer, or dimension, of digital literacy, requiring educators to understand not only how AI 
operates within these systems but also the broader social, ethical, and educational implications of 
its use.  
The emergence and evolution of AI technologies have prompted researchers to consider 
the concept of AI literacy (Ng et al., 2021). According to Laupichler et al. (2022), the term “AI 
literacy” was noted back in 2015 and appeared in academic literature soon after. Nevertheless, 
beginning around 2022, the advent of publicly accessible Large Language Models (LLMs) such 
as OpenAI’s ChatGPT led to a quick expansion of AI literacy, especially as these technologies 
rapidly entered the world of education (Almatrafi et al., 2024; Pinski & Benlian, 2024; Van Wyk, 
2025). Generally, AI literacy today is seen across four broad domains: know and understand, use 
and apply, evaluate and create, and ethical issues (Ng et al., 2021). 
AI literacy involves grasping the basic principles of AI, including how AI systems work, 
their capabilities, and limitations, along with how teachers/students may utilize these technologies 
(Chiu et al., 2024). This involves a foundational understanding of AI functions (machine learning, 
algorithmic decision-making, and data analytics) that, in turn, enables learners to evaluate and 
demystify the capabilities and limitations of AI-based educational tools. Further, AI literacy 
involves knowing how to effectively use AI tools and technologies in various contexts, including 
personal and professional settings. Hence, for teachers and TCs, the application component of AI 
literacy focuses on the practical integration of AI tools in education. This dimension requires 
educators to thoughtfully incorporate AI-driven applications, such as adaptive learning platforms 
and automated assessment tools, in ways that align with instructional objectives and support 
individualized learning.  
AI literacy also includes the ability to critically assess AI technologies and understand their 
ethical implications, biases, and potential impact on students and society, particularly in the areas 
of data privacy, security, and algorithmic bias. As AI technologies increasingly collect and analyze 
student data, educators must recognize potential ethical challenges, including issues around 
consent, data rights, and algorithmic discrimination (Casal-Otero et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2025; 
Zhou et al., 2025).

effective teaching with technology as the intersection of three knowledge domains: content (CK), 
pedagogy (PK), and technology (TK) (Mishra & Koehler, 2006). As AI becomes increasingly

embedded in educational contexts, new models of AI literacy have emerged to extend and 
complement TPACK (Mishra et al., 2023).  
Hence, there appears to be a concurrent movement towards merging TPACK with AI 
literacy, forming updated frameworks such as AI Literacy-TPACK framework (Ng et al. 2021) 
and the Intelligent-TPACK framework (Celik, 2023) to prepare teachers for AI-enhanced teaching 
environments. This fusion requires designing pedagogy that promotes critical engagement with 
AI. The AI Literacy-TPACK framework proposed by Ng et al. (2021) integrates technological, 
pedagogical, and content knowledge to guide educators in developing AI literacy across diverse 
learning contexts. Technological Knowledge is addressed through a range of learning artifacts, 
including hardware, software, AI-related agents, unplugged activities and gamified tools to support 
forms of hands-on and engaging learning. Pedagogically, the framework promotes diverse 
approaches such as inquiry-based learning, collaborative projects, constructionism, and problem-
based learning, with adjustments made for learners’ developmental stages. Content knowledge 
focuses on two key dimensions: conceptual understanding of AI (e.g., algorithms, machine 
learning) and human-centred ethical considerations (e.g., bias, accountability). The model 
emphasizes that AI literacy is not merely technical proficiency, but a multidimensional 
competency requiring critical awareness, ethical engagement, and pedagogical adaptability. Thus, 
the AI Literacy-TPACK framework serves as an entry point to considering how teachers, including 
new TCs, may begin to better understand GenAI within the context of AIEd (Ng et al., 2021). 
In this study, AI literacy was operationalized by extending established notions of digital 
literacy to the emerging demands of AI-enhanced educational contexts. More specifically, to 
situate these competencies within professional practice, the AI Literacy-TPACK framework was 
used as a guiding model that integrates technological, pedagogical, and content knowledge to 
capture how TCs navigate AI in relation to curriculum and pedagogy. This enabled a structured 
lens for analyzing both technical proficiency and critical engagement, positioning AI literacy as a 
multidimensional construct relevant to teacher preparation in the age of GenAI. 
 
Methodology 
 
The study adopted a multiple-case study approach (Yin, 2014), with each case representing a 
cohort of TCs enrolled in a digital technology course in a pre-service teacher education program 
at a university in Ontario, Canada. Within this framework, an explanatory mixed-methods design 
(Creswell & Creswell, 2023) was employed, combining quantitative and qualitative data collected 
via a survey administered to TCs directly following their completion of the AI module. Ethical 
clearance was granted by the Research Ethics Board at the university to conduct this research. 
 
Research setting 
 
The study occurred in a 12-week online course on digital technologies. This course is 
offered in the first year of the two-year teacher education program. As per the course description, 
the purpose of this course is to prepare TCs for a technology-enhanced classroom. The course 
focuses on research-based strategies for effective integration of technologies across the 
curriculum. It identifies the knowledge and skills that teachers need to competently teach with 
technology while providing opportunities for enhancing personal skills with technology tools 
currently being used in Ontario classrooms. As such, the course provided a convenient medium to 
incorporate concepts related to AIEd.

The AIEd module 
 
As of 2024, the instructional team of this course has dedicated a three-hour module on 
AIEd (Estaiteyeh et al., 2025). The two authors of this paper collaborated on the design and 
development of this module. The aim of the module is to provide an overarching understanding of 
AI tools and an introduction to AI literacy. Specifically, the objectives of the module are to: 
• Provide an overview of AI and its relevance in education. 
• Discuss the affordances and implications of AI tools for education, including 
personalized learning, adaptive assessments, and content creation. 
• Address concerns related to AI in education (ethical, privacy, academic integrity, etc.), 
emphasizing the responsible use of AI tools by TCs and their future students. 
• Examine various AI tools and applications that can enhance teaching and learning in 
intermediate-senior (I/S) classes. 
For the two cohorts in this study, the module began with an overview of AI, providing 
definitions of important concepts such as AI, GenAI, and LLMs. This was followed by hands-on 
activities on content creation using AI tools, such as creating images and videos. Afterwards, TCs 
were engaged in group discussions on the affordances, limitations, and ethical implications of these 
tools. The second half of this module focused on the pedagogical implications and applications of 
AI. TCs examined various AI tools and applications for creating educational content, such as 
lesson plans, unit plans, teaching resources, and assessment tools. For example, TCs explored 
Eduaide and Magic School to develop lesson plans, unit plans, and teaching resources; Diffit to 
facilitate differentiated instruction; Quizziz for creating assessments; and Microsoft coaches as an 
example of intelligent tutoring and adaptive assessment tools.   
Moreover, as a class activity, TCs were tasked with creating lesson plans using Eduaide 
and ChatGPT. As part of the activity instructions, TCs asked the applications to revise the AI-
generated lesson plans multiple times. TCs were encouraged to chat with the tools and not take the 
first generated output. TCs did the same for creating a formative assessment tool, a summative 
assessment tool, and a rubric for assessment. Afterwards, groups of TCs critiqued the generated 
output by answering the following prompts: 
• Evaluate the generated products: Provide a short evaluation of the pros and cons. 
• What is missing in your lesson plan? What can be improved? 
• What are some barriers to learning in this lesson? 
• What else should we consider? 
• Reflect on the process of using AI tools to generate lesson plans and educational 
content. 
Following their group discussions, TCs presented their critique and discussed their 
reflections with the whole class. TCs were asked to highlight the differences between AI-generated 
and teacher-generated lesson plans. TCs were encouraged to be critical by focusing on the 
limitations of AI-generated content. Finally, the module wrapped up with a teacher-led 
presentation on the most recent trends in AI applications, affordances, and limitations. The 
presentation also included a section on AI literacy definition, current frameworks, and importance, 
as well as practical ways to promote students’ AI literacy. The module included many resources 
for TCs to explore later, including tutorials, online courses, articles, guidebooks, and suggested 
applications for future teaching.

Participants 
 
Participants in Cohort 1 were 108 TCs who completed the course in 2024. Participants in 
Cohort 2 were 104 TCs who completed the course in 2025. All TCs were in the intermediate-senior 
(I/S) division with teachable subjects in various disciplines (sciences, math, languages, social 
studies, etc.). TCs in this division typically teach two subjects within intermediate and secondary 
classes upon program completion. 
 
Data sources 
 
Quantitative and qualitative data were collected using a survey administered online to TCs 
right after the AI module completion, using the Qualtrics survey system. This paper reports on 
three Likert scale statements and two open-ended questions. The survey statements and questions 
addressed TCs’ experiences with the module on AIEd and their views on the use of AI tools. The 
Likert scale items were: 
• To what extent was this session helpful to you in exploring topics related to AI in 
education? Not effective at all (1) - Extremely effective (5) 
• How do you feel about using AI in your future teaching? Extremely uncomfortable (1) 
- Extremely comfortable (5) 
• How do you feel about your students using AI in their work in the future? Extremely 
uncomfortable (1) - Extremely comfortable (5) 
The two open-ended questions, developed by the research team based on the literature and 
course activities, include: 
• What are your main takeaways from this session? 
• What topics would you like to explore further in relation to AI in Education? 
 
Data analysis 
 
Qualtrics and Microsoft Excel were used to analyze the quantitative data resulting from 
TCs’ responses to Likert scale statements. This analysis included calculating counts, averages, 
standard deviations, and percentages. These measures provided a baseline understanding of the 
extent to which participants found the session helpful and how comfortable they felt about using 
AI in their own teaching as well as in their future students’ work. Comparisons across cohorts were 
conducted to identify consistencies or variations in responses between the two groups.  
As for the qualitative data resulting from TCs’ responses to open-ended questions, an 
inductive thematic analysis (Gall et al., 2005) was conducted. The two authors synthesized initial 
codes based on the frequency count in TCs’ responses. Thereafter, the authors grouped similar 
codes into themes to draw conclusions. To ensure coding consistency and reduce researcher bias, 
the authors collaboratively compared their coding decisions and discussed discrepancies until 
consensus was reached. This process functioned as an informal inter-rater reliability check. 
Themes were then refined through iterative review, ensuring they accurately represented the data 
and aligned with the study’s research questions. Representative quotations from TCs were selected 
to illustrate the range and depth of perspectives within each theme.  
Finally, the quantitative and qualitative findings were integrated and analyzed from the 
lens of the theoretical framework to provide a more comprehensive understanding of TCs’ 
experiences with the AIEd module and developing views on AIEd and AI literacy.

Results 
 
TCs’ experiences with the AIEd module  
 
TCs were asked to rate the extent to which the AI module was helpful to them in exploring 
topics related to AI in education on a scale of one (not effective at all) to five (extremely effective). 
The average rating was 4.2 (SD=0.7) for the first cohort and 3.7 (SD=1.0) for the second cohort, 
which indicates a high level of satisfaction with the AIEd module in Cohort 1 and a moderate level 
of satisfaction in Cohort 2. Specifically, in 2024 (first cohort), 33% of TCs indicated that the 
module was extremely effective, and 53% indicated it was very effective. Similarly, in 2025 
(second cohort), 19% indicated that the module was extremely effective, 43% indicated it was very 
effective, and 28% moderately effective.  
Qualitative comments reiterated that students held overall positive views of the AIEd 
module. When asked about their main takeaways from the module, three main themes emerged 
from TCs’ responses: offering useful teaching resources, more acceptance to explore the 
technology, and promoting AI literacy. First, many TCs emphasized how the module offered new 
resources for lesson planning, teaching, and assessment. Those TCs highlighted the abilities of 
some applications in generating educational resources. They said: 
 
I had never used an AI tool before, and I didn't realize how effective it could be at making 
an in-depth lesson plan. (TC46, Cohort 1) 
 
Really enjoyed this lesson. Amazing to think about all the AI tools that exist today - for 
better or for worse. (TC60, Cohort 1) 
 
Second, several TCs indicated that they left the module with more acceptance to explore 
the technology and better attitudes to embrace it (critically). For example: 
 
I think that the main takeaway from this session, along with discussions with other students, 
is that AI is a brave new world but can actually work as something we should embrace 
rather than shy away from. This can help save time on lesson creation time by providing a 
great base, but not a final product. (TC7, Cohort1) 
 
AI and its capabilities are very vast, there are a lot of pros but also a lot of cons, and as a 
teacher, it is up to us to educate ourselves and seek the education we need to learn about it. 
(TC14, Cohort1) 
 
Finally, TCs highlighted concepts related to AI literacy, as it relates to them as teachers or 
their students. TCs indicated that the module offered them skills to use certain AI tools effectively 
and knowledge of the abilities and limitations of AIEd applications. With respect to students, TCs 
mentioned the importance of developing students’ AI literacy and enabling them to use AI tools 
effectively and ethically. TCs said: 
 
AI can be an extremely useful tool, but you need to know how to use it properly. It also 
takes more than one prompt to get the outcome we want. We should utilize several prompts 
and not settle for the first one. (TC124, Cohort 2)

their students using AI. In Cohort 1, around 47% of TCs expressed comfort with their students 
using AI compared to only 30% in Cohort 2. Overall, the results show TCs’ positive views of AI 
and possibly an inclination to use it as teachers compared to their negative views toward their 
students using AI in their work. It is also worth noting that TCs’ views on AI use were generally 
more positive in Cohort 1 compared to the more recent cohort (Cohort 2).

Discussion 
 
TCs’ general satisfaction with the AIEd module highlighted three major benefits: offering useful 
resources for lesson planning, teaching, and assessment; more acceptance to explore the 
technology and better attitudes to embrace it critically; and promoting AI literacy. This finding 
reiterates the importance of ongoing professional development of TCs in AIEd topics to promote 
their AI literacy and preparedness for their future careers (Hopcan et al., 2024; Ng et al., 202; 
Sanusi et al., 2024; Trust et al., 2024; Uygun et al., 2024; Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019). 
Specifically, such professional development can be integrated within existing educational 
technology courses and engage TCs in critical discussions on AIEd (Chiu et al, 2025; Guan et al., 
2025; Hur, 2005; Kohnke, 2023). 
With respect to their views on AI use, TCs in the study appeared to grasp the complexity 
of integrating GenAI into their developing pedagogical practices, often framing it as a balancing 
act between innovation and responsibility. While they acknowledged the utility of AI for 
generating initial drafts, lesson outlines, and teaching materials, they emphasized that final

decisions, content curation, and contextual adjustments should remain within the educator-
controlled domain. This perhaps reflects a recognition among new teachers that GenAI can 
support, but not replace, the professional judgment and ethical discernment that define effective 
teaching.  
A recurring theme was the duality teachers now face: to use GenAI responsibly within both 
the positions of a student and a (future) teacher. Across both cohorts, a common thread appears 
where TCs recognize the apparent inevitability of GenAI in educational contexts, yet remain 
uncertain about its overall usage, value, and how to navigate its pedagogical and ethical 
implications. Such ideas and concerns are not peripheral as they reflect a fundamental shift in the 
epistemological and operational structures of teaching, whereby knowledge production, 
assessment/evaluation and content-area expertise and pedagogy are increasingly mediated by 
algorithmic systems and forms of AI. 
These perspectives align with broader debates in the field of AIEd, where there is currently 
little consensus on best practices, frameworks, or guidelines for implementation (Holmes et al., 
2022; Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019), and TCs' responses across both years of the study reflected 
this uncertainty. Concerns about student misuse of AI were frequently raised, including fears of 
plagiarism, superficial engagement, and diminished critical thinking (Kumar & Sharma, 2025). At 
the same time, TCs expressed interest in practical strategies, such as using AI to design 
assessments or support marking, as well as in tools to detect misuse. This divergence in priorities 
revealed differences in depth and orientation, highlighting the need for clear guidance, which is 
concurrently essential (Estaiteyeh, 2025; Ghimire & Edwards, 2024). What emerged from TCs’ 
reflections was an ongoing tension where today, both teachers and in-training TCs are 
simultaneously navigating the push toward educational innovation and the need to preserve 
academic integrity, authenticity, and meaningful learning. Despite these tensions, there was a 
shared sense that GenAI is not a passing trend but a permanent fixture in the future of education.  
Finally, TCs demonstrated developing levels of AI literacy, especially among the most 
recent cohort of TCs, as they were aware of the benefits of AI applications but were skeptical given 
the associated negative implications (Ng et al., 2021; UNESCO, 2024). Accordingly, TCs 
recognized the need to adapt, not by uncritically adopting new tools, but by developing the critical 
and ethical fluency with AI tools. These findings also emphasize the need for updated frameworks 
that complement TPACK, such as AI Literacy-TPACK framework (Ng et al. 2021) and the 
Intelligent-TPACK framework (Celik, 2023), to better prepare teachers for AI-enhanced teaching 
and learning environments. From a policy perspective, the findings illustrate that AI literacy should 
be further integrated into teacher preparation curricula as well as for in-service teachers, to match 
the rapid evolution of GenAI technologies and their educational applications (Sperling et al., 
2024). 
 
Conclusion and Implications 
 
TCs continue to grapple with AIEd issues just as other educators have in recent years. This 
indicates the ongoing importance of developing relevant and effective AIEd content, discussions, 
and professional development in teacher education programs. AI literacy is likely no longer just 
an addendum to teacher preparation, but instead a more critical dimension of professional 
competence of new teachers. Its inclusion in teacher education programs is essential to ensure that 
educators are prepared to understand AI and its implications; use AI effectively in ways that uphold 
pedagogical innovation, academic integrity, and authentic learning; and respond to the evolving

realities of their profession. To do so, TCs must be equipped with both the conceptual and practical 
skills required to interrogate the technologies they are expected to use. 
This research advances knowledge in AIEd within the context of teacher education. It 
informs teacher educators, researchers, and program leaders about how to better prepare AI-literate 
educators for the classroom of the future. The research also informs policymakers about the 
importance of AI literacy in teacher education programs and the need for clear policies and 
guidelines that address both in-service and pre-service teachers' concerns around AIEd. 
Future research can delve deeper into TCs’ experiences in similar professional 
development programs by offering prolonged training and exploring how such training impacts 
specific outcomes within AI literacy frameworks for educators. Further, longitudinal research is 
needed to follow up with TCs on their use of AI and its implications as they start their teaching 
career. Finally, there seems to be a need for more theoretical conceptualization to frame empirical 
studies on AIEd, especially in the context of teacher education.
