PLN & the Evolving Internet

Andy HuMarch 2026Blog Post #7 · Week 9
My Network

Current PLN Landscape

My PLN currently revolves around three clusters. The first is the developer community - GitHub, Stack Overflow, tech Twitter, and a handful of Discord servers where people share tools, patterns, and problems. This is where most of my professional learning happens in real time. When I hit a technical challenge, the answer almost always comes from somewhere in this network before it shows up in official documentation.

The second cluster is the product and design community - a mix of LinkedIn connections, Substack newsletters, and a few curated feeds on BlueSky. This is where I follow people who think about the intersection of technology and user experience. The third cluster, which I only started building since this course began, includes educators and people working in EdTech - the Mattermost community here at UVic, a few education-focused accounts I now follow, and resources like Chris Kennedy's blog on educational leadership in BC.

Kennedy is a useful example of how PLNs work in Canadian education. As superintendent of the West Vancouver School District, he has maintained a public blog and social media presence for years, sharing reflections on educational leadership, innovation, and the challenges of running a public school system. His work demonstrates that a PLN is not just a personal tool - it can model transparency and reflective practice for an entire organization. In the Canadian context, this kind of visible educational leadership through social media remains relatively uncommon compared to the US, which makes the examples that exist more valuable.

Critical Assessment

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths
  • Deep technical expertise within the network
  • Fast, real-time problem-solving through community
  • Strong professional identity across platforms
  • Open-source culture encourages knowledge sharing
  • Direct access to industry leaders and practitioners
Weaknesses
  • Heavy tech bias - limited perspectives outside the industry
  • Low geographic diversity - mostly North American voices
  • Minimal engagement with education and social sciences
  • Consumption-heavy on some platforms (YouTube, Reddit)
  • AI tools integrated without enough critical assessment

The biggest gap in my PLN is diversity of discipline. Almost everyone in my network is a developer, designer, or product person. That means the perspectives I am exposed to are strong on implementation but weak on the societal implications of what we build. Following educators, policy researchers, and people working in fields like accessibility and public health would give me a more complete picture of how the things I build affect people who use them.

Canadian Context

AI Literacy & the PLN

Estaiteyeh and Mindzak (2025) studied how Canadian teacher candidates engage with AI literacy, and their findings are directly relevant to how PLNs evolve. Among two cohorts of pre-service teachers in Ontario, the majority found value in learning about AI - not because they wanted to become AI experts, but because they recognized that their future students would be using these tools whether educators prepared for it or not. The study found that teacher candidates were generally inclined to use AI themselves but expressed negative views toward their students using it, which reveals a tension that most PLNs have not resolved yet.

That tension shows up in my own network. I use AI tools daily - code generation, debugging, writing assistance, research. I do not question whether these tools are useful. What I have not done enough of is critically evaluating how they reshape my learning process. Am I still building knowledge, or am I outsourcing the thinking that would lead to knowledge? That question is not comfortable, and most of my PLN does not engage with it. The tech community tends to treat AI adoption as inevitable progress rather than something worth examining critically.

Broadening my PLN to include voices from education research - people like Estaiteyeh and Mindzak who study how practitioners actually engage with AI, rather than just how the technology works - would make my relationship with these tools more informed. The strongest PLNs are not the ones with the most connections. They are the ones that include people who make you question assumptions you did not know you were making. For me, the assumption I need to question most right now is that more AI integration always equals better outcomes. The Canadian research suggests it is more complicated than that.
