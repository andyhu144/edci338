Who Needs to Know About Your PLN?

Andy HuMarch 2026Blog Post #6 · Week 8
Qualman (2012)

Empowering Others Through Your Network

Qualman's central argument in "Empower Others" is that digital leadership is not about building the biggest following. It is about creating value for the people in your network. That resonates with how I think about professional reputation. The people who have helped me most in my career were not trying to build a personal brand. They were genuinely sharing what they knew because it helped them think more clearly about their own work. The sharing was a byproduct of their learning process, not a marketing strategy.

In my company, I have seen this play out directly. The team members who share what they learn — a debugging technique, a design pattern, a client interaction that went wrong — create more value for the organization than the ones who keep their knowledge private and protected. Empowerment is not about being generous with your time. It is about recognizing that knowledge compounds when it circulates. Hoarding it creates a bottleneck. Sharing it creates a multiplier.

Course Reflection

What Has Shifted So Far

Halfway through this course, the biggest shift in my thinking is about intentionality. Before, I used social media platforms reactively — post when something was worth sharing, scroll when I had time, follow people when I stumbled across their content. Now I think about my PLN as a system I am actively designing. Who am I learning from? What gaps exist in the perspectives I am exposed to? Am I producing content that contributes to my professional community or just consuming content from it?

The V&R mapping exercise from week 3 was the first time I actually laid out my digital engagement visually. Seeing it as a map — rather than just a list of accounts I have — made the patterns obvious. I am heavily resident in professional spaces and mostly a visitor in personal ones. That is fine for career purposes, but it means my PLN skews toward people who think like me. The inclusion discussion in week 4 pushed me to consider whether I am learning from genuinely diverse voices or just from people in my industry who happen to live in different cities.

The media literacy week was valuable in a different way. I already understood the concept of misinformation. What I did not have was a vocabulary for the economic structures behind it. Hirst's framework — fake news as a business model, not just an information failure — gave me a way to think about why misinformation persists even when fact-checking tools exist. The incentives are misaligned. Engagement metrics reward outrage, and outrage is easier to generate with false or misleading content than with careful, accurate reporting.

My perspective on social media in professional settings has not changed dramatically — I was already careful about separating personal and professional presence. What has changed is my understanding of why that separation matters. It is not just about risk management. It is about designing an identity that accurately represents what you want to be known for, while remaining honest about who you actually are.

Professional Practice

How I Will Use This

Going forward, I plan to continue with my current professional content — technical writing, open-source contributions, product updates — but with more deliberate curation of what I consume. I want to follow more educators, more designers from outside the tech industry, and more people whose work sits at the intersection of technology and public good. My PLN has been functional but narrow. Making it broader is a design problem, and now I have frameworks to approach it as one.

Hermida's argument in "Tell Everyone" is that sharing is a fundamental human behavior that social media has amplified, not created. We share to process our own thinking, to help others, and to strengthen our social bonds. In a professional context, that means the content I share is not just about building a brand. It is about contributing to a community of practice where knowledge moves freely and the people who share the most end up learning the most. That is the PLN I want to build — one where the act of sharing is the learning, not just a channel for distributing what I already know.
