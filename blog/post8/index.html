AI & Social Media

Andy HuMarch 2026Blog Post #8 · Week 10
Risks & Opportunities

AI-Driven Misinformation

The instructor made a point this week that stuck with me: "doomscrolling now includes AI slop." That is a good summary of where we are. AI-generated content has reached the point where it is not just filling gaps in content production — it is flooding social media feeds with material that looks real, sounds authoritative, and requires genuine effort to evaluate. For digital citizenship, that changes the baseline. It is no longer enough to teach people to check sources. You now need to teach them to question whether the content they are looking at was produced by a human at all.

MediaSmarts highlights this in their Media Literacy Week report: the volume of AI-generated content online has grown faster than the public's ability to critically assess it. The gap between production capacity and literacy is the core problem. AI can generate thousands of articles, images, and videos in the time it takes a human to evaluate one. That asymmetry makes traditional fact-checking insufficient as a primary defense. The scale of the problem requires systemic responses, not just individual vigilance.

Canadian Context

Extremism & AI in Canada

A specific Canadian example is the CBC investigation that uncovered extremist activity within the Canadian military, including the use of online platforms and AI-generated content to recruit and radicalize. The investigation revealed that white supremacist networks were operating with relative impunity inside military structures, using social media to spread ideology and coordinate. Groups like the Canadian Anti-Hate Network (antihate.ca) identified these patterns well before government agencies acted on them — demonstrating that civil society organizations are often faster at detecting AI-amplified radicalization than the institutions responsible for preventing it.

Vass Bednar's article in The Walrus frames this within a broader cultural context. "AI slop" — low-quality, synthetic content flooding digital platforms — threatens to drown the human voices that Canadian cultural policy was specifically designed to protect. Canada's broadcasting and cultural funding frameworks were built on the premise that Canadian stories need protection from dominant American media. AI-generated content introduces a new threat: not foreign cultural dominance, but algorithmic irrelevance. When AI can produce infinite content at zero cost, the economic model that supports Canadian journalism, art, and media production becomes increasingly fragile.

The impact on public opinion is not hypothetical. When AI-generated content is indistinguishable from human-created content, and when engagement algorithms distribute both equally, the entire information environment becomes unreliable. Public opinion is not formed by individual articles. It is shaped by the aggregate of what people see, and when a significant portion of that aggregate is synthetic, the foundation of informed democratic participation erodes. This is particularly concerning in the Canadian context, where the DAIS report on Canada's AI strategy emphasizes the need for proactive regulation rather than reactive responses.

Proposal

Media Literacy Curriculum Segment

I would propose a three-module media literacy segment aimed at secondary school students (grades 10–12) that focuses specifically on AI-generated content. The goal is not to make students afraid of AI. It is to give them the critical framework to evaluate any content they encounter, regardless of its source.

Proposed Curriculum: Critical AI Literacy
1
Detection How to identify AI-generated text, images, and video. Hands-on exercises comparing human and synthetic content.
2
Economics Who profits from misinformation? Follow the money from content creation through ad revenue to platform engagement.
3
Response What to do when you encounter misinformation. Reporting, not sharing, lateral reading, and upstream verification.

This curriculum structure is supported by DAIS's recommendation that Canada's AI strategy needs to address literacy gaps alongside regulatory frameworks. Regulation alone cannot solve the problem because the content evolves faster than policy. Building critical assessment skills at the secondary level creates a population that can adapt to new forms of misinformation as they emerge. The investment is in the capacity to think critically, not in the ability to detect any specific type of content.
