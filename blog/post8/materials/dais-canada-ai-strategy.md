## Executive Summary

This submission proposes a renewed Canadian AI strategy built on three pillars: responsible adoption with trust-based governance, workforce talent development, and sovereign AI infrastructure. The analysis draws from over 25 Dais reports examining AI adoption, skills, compute, and governance.

While Canada pioneered the first national AI strategy in 2017, leadership in research hasn't translated into broad economic adoption or productivity gains. AI adoption remains concentrated among large firms (25.9% versus 5.9% in small businesses as of 2023), public trust remains low, and talent retention faces challenges from US compensation gaps.

## Key Findings

### AI Adoption Barriers
- Only approximately 7% of Canadian firms have integrated AI into operations
- Knowledge gaps represent the primary adoption barrier, not lack of utility
- Nonprofit sector largely excluded from AI strategy despite 170,000+ organizations
- Federal public servants face higher AI-exposure occupations (59% versus 29% nationally)

### Trust and Governance
- Canadians rank among the world's most skeptical regarding AI technologies
- Self-governance by tech companies proves insufficient for protecting users
- Existing regulatory frameworks can address AI harms without entirely novel legislation

### Talent and Skills
- Canadian tech workers earn approximately 46% less than US counterparts
- Women comprise only one-fifth of tech workers, unchanged for two decades
- Canada ranks 44th globally in AI training availability
- K-12 educators lack resources and guidance for teaching AI literacy

### Sovereignty Challenges
- Canada lags all G7 nations in publicly-owned compute resources
- Heavy reliance on foreign cloud providers threatens competitiveness

## Major Recommendations (Selected)

**Pillar 1: Responsible Adoption & Governance**
- Develop programming addressing knowledge gaps for low-adoption organization leaders
- Task regulators with assessing how existing regimes apply to AI
- Prioritize international governance coordination with values-aligned democracies

**Pillar 2: Talent & Skills**
- Develop a comprehensive AI literacy strategy for all ages
- Support Pan-Canadian AI literacy framework development for K-12 education
- Grow AI research talent while increasing women's participation

**Pillar 3: Sovereignty**
- Build sovereign compute capacity for strategic priority applications
- Develop public AI models emphasizing Canadian ownership
