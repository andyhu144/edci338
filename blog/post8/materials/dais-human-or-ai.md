## Executive Summary

The report examines how social media platforms label AI-generated content and whether these labels effectively inform users.

### Main Discoveries

- Small disclaimer labels are "functionally no different from leaving content entirely unlabeled" in terms of user trust or sharing behavior
- Full-screen blocking labels are most effective, reducing exposure and improving user perception of labeling effectiveness
- 47% of Canadian residents encounter deepfakes weekly; 20% see them multiple times daily
- TikTok users (78%) and Instagram users (76%) report highest exposure to synthetic content
- Older Canadians struggle identifying AI-generated material compared to younger demographics
- Canada lacks statutory governance frameworks, relying instead on voluntary platform measures

## Key Research Findings

**Survey Methodology:** Online survey of 2,472 Canadian residents (16+) conducted September-November 2024, with experiment testing three labeling approaches on mock Facebook feeds.

### Deepfake Awareness
- 35% of ages 16-29 report high familiarity; only 12% of those over 60 do
- Despite familiarity claims, only 45% could accurately define deepfakes
- Just 32% spontaneously mentioned AI when describing deepfakes

### Exposure Patterns
- 70% encounter deepfakes at least monthly
- YouTube users show highest daily exposure
- Political ideology influences familiarity

### Labeling Effectiveness
- Small labels: 39% found effective; performed identically to unlabeled content
- Full-screen labels: 60% found effective; reduced visibility from 46-67% to 39-43%
- Neither approach changed user credibility judgments or sharing intentions

## Regulatory Context

**Canada's Status:** Bills C-27 (Artificial Intelligence and Data Act) and C-63 (Online Harms Act) stalled before parliament's prorogation in January 2025, leaving no federal statutory framework.

**International Comparison:** The EU's Artificial Intelligence Act (effective August 2024) mandates that AI developers label synthetic outputs via watermarking or equivalent methods.

## Recommendations

1. Statutory requirements similar to EU standards
2. More assertive labeling interventions (full-screen blocking rather than disclaimers)
3. Platform accountability mechanisms for synthetic media identification
4. Public education about AI-generated content, especially targeting older demographics
