Canadian Privacy Law

PIPEDA & the Patchwork Problem

PIPEDA - the Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act - is the federal framework governing how private-sector organizations collect, use, and disclose personal information in Canada. As someone who runs a software company that handles user data, I deal with PIPEDA requirements directly. The principles are straightforward in theory: get consent before collecting, collect only what is necessary, use it only for the stated purpose, keep it accurate, and let people access and correct their own data.

In practice, the landscape is more complicated. PIPEDA applies to the private sector at the federal level, but provinces like British Columbia, Alberta, and Quebec have their own substantially similar legislation. BC's FIPPA (Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act) governs public bodies including universities. This patchwork means that data flowing through educational PLNs can be subject to different privacy regimes depending on where the platform is hosted, where the institution is located, and whether the data crosses provincial or national borders.

Data Harvesting

The Hidden Cost of PLN Platforms

Collection Is personal data being collected without explicit educational purpose?
Compliance Are third-party analytics compliant with Canadian privacy standards?
Control Do students have meaningful control over their information?

Every PLN platform is a data relationship. When you build a professional network on LinkedIn, you are creating value for yourself - connections, visibility, professional development opportunities. You are also creating value for LinkedIn - your professional data becomes part of their dataset about industry patterns, hiring trends, and career trajectories. That data is packaged and sold to recruiters, advertisers, and enterprise clients. The platform's business model depends on harvesting exactly the kind of professional information that makes a PLN valuable.

The ethical question is not whether this trade-off is acceptable. It is whether users - especially students - have enough information to make an informed decision about it. In most educational contexts, students are directed to use specific platforms without a meaningful discussion of what data those platforms collect, where it is stored, and who profits from it. The DAIS report on generative AI and youth privacy raises this concern specifically: privacy frameworks like PIPEDA were not designed for an environment where AI systems analyze behavioral data at scale to build predictive profiles of users, including minors.

The instructor's point about Mattermost versus WhatsApp is relevant here. Using an institutionally hosted communication platform means the data stays within a controlled environment where institutional privacy policies apply. Shifting to WhatsApp or iMessage moves the data onto commercial platforms governed by different privacy frameworks - often US-based, where the privacy protections are fundamentally different from Canada's. This is not a hypothetical concern. It is a daily operational decision that has real privacy implications, and it becomes more significant when the data involves student participation, grades, and personal reflections.

Integration

Connecting to the Final Project

For the final project, the privacy dimension is not a separate topic to address. It is embedded in every recommendation we make about PLN platforms. If the project recommends that professionals use LinkedIn to build their network, it also needs to acknowledge that LinkedIn monetizes the professional data that makes the network valuable. If it recommends using Google Docs for collaboration, it needs to note that Google's free tier is subsidized by data collection. These are not reasons to avoid these tools. They are reasons to use them with informed consent and clear-eyed awareness of the trade-offs.

In my own work, PIPEDA compliance shapes product decisions daily. Where we host data, how we obtain consent, what we collect and what we do not - these are not afterthoughts. They are design constraints that influence architecture from the beginning. The same thinking should apply to educational technology decisions. The question is not just "does this tool work?" It is "does this tool handle our students' data in a way that meets our obligations?" That is the lens I will bring to the final project.
