### Part 2: Interview with Sophie Lui (Global BC News Anchor)

**Sophie Lui:** So I'm a news anchor at Global BC. I host the five o'clock and six o'clock news. That's my current job with Global. I've been here for quite a long time. I've been mostly an anchor over the years that I've been here. I've also reported over the years, but I've been a journalist for more than 20 years. I graduated from BCIT in 1995. I worked in radio for about five years, in news radio as a reporter and as a desker, is what we call it in radio. A desker is basically an anchor. And then after about five years in radio, I moved to TV. I worked at CHEK TV in Victoria, and here at Global. And I sort of went back and forth a couple of times. And this is my second time here at Global.

**Jesse Miller:** We grew up without the advantage of having cell phones and social media, not only in the sense of our connected lives, but also just in that everyday news access. What were your first couple years like in radio and TV without an iPhone?

**Sophie Lui:** Well, you just reminded me. So my very first radio news job was at a station in Kelowna called CKIQ. And we had a newsroom cell phone, so the reporter could take out the cell phone. But it was one of those big brick cell phones. Huge, heavy things. And I was out on the beat one day, and I had a stack of papers and the brick cell phone with me in the news cruiser, and I was very new, a cub reporter, very flustered. I put the stuff on top of the car — the file folders and the cell phone. And then I got in the car and drove off. Of course, all the papers flew off the top of my car, but the cell phone stayed. Because that's how heavy it was.

Back then we would use phone books, like actual phone books. And if I was doing a story on, say, renovations booming because people are spending so much time at home during the pandemic, I would use the Yellow Pages to look up an interior designer or a contractor. Whereas these days, we would now Google that. So phone books were definitely our friends back then. The reverse directory was a great resource — you could look up by phone number who had that phone number and you could track people down that way.

**Jesse Miller:** Being in media, and migrating from radio, TV, Victoria to Vancouver, in the early part of your career, what was your network like of individuals that you could rely on?

**Sophie Lui:** It's been a long time since I applied for a job, but back then it would be a lot of hustle. It would be about cold-calling people, and I mean actually calling them as opposed to emailing them. It would be going to whatever town it was that I wanted to maybe work in. It would be about making sure I was at an event where I might be able to shake someone's hand, or get my face in front of someone. It would be sending tapes — actual tapes. I had radio demo reels and TV demo reels as well. It would be following up. Like sending something and then actually picking up the phone and saying, "Hey, it's Sophie Lui, I sent you a tape. I'm just wondering if you've had a chance to look at it or listen to it." And, "I'd love to meet you for coffee."

I did that not just with prospective employers, but also with people in the business who were much more senior than me, and who I thought might be able to offer advice. Like Jill Krop, who is my current boss. When I was at CHEK TV, she was at BCTV here in Vancouver, which is now Global. And I looked up to her, and I asked her if I could meet her for coffee or lunch, and just pick her brain.

**Jesse Miller:** You are now part of such a strong female voice of reporters in British Columbia. When we see these unfortunate events of live hits that are interrupted by misogynistic terminology, we see all these reporters kind of come together. Did you find in the early part of your career that a person like Jill Krop was receptive to a young female reporter coming out of a similar program?

**Sophie Lui:** Yeah, she was very receptive. We went out for lunch. There was also Marissa Adair, who's no longer in the business. Ted Fields, who I work with now — when I was a student I shadowed him for a day. He was great.

In terms of the sisterhood, or the women supporting women, yes, there was that support, but it wasn't quite as big a network as it is now. There was a woman in Kelowna or Victoria who had a viewer comment on her low-cut blouse, which wasn't low-cut at all, and who even cares? I don't know her, but I definitely feel like, no, that's not right, and she's one of me, and if she has to take this, I'm gonna step up for her.

And same with Mithu. Mithu Garcha, our current morning show host, really took a lot of heat for an interview she did a few days ago with John Horgan. Criticism is one thing, but this stuff about, "She was rude," and, "She's young and inexperienced," and it's bullshit, frankly. She's one of my people, so I'm not one to just sit on the sidelines and let that happen.

**Jesse Miller:** In the sense of that social media network, do you think that 20 years ago prior to Twitter, that positive reinforcement would have not really been in the public eye?

**Sophie Lui:** I don't know if the public would necessarily know all this stuff without that. So I guess in a way it's good that they can see the challenges we have to face. Although I don't know if it encourages some of the jerks out there to continue doing it too. I do hear from people outside of the business, "Oh, I can't believe you have to put up with that kind of stuff."

Even before social media, you'd still get letters. I used to get actual letters mailed to me. Phone calls — and viewers didn't realize that I answered the phone, so when they called to complain about me, it was me on the other line picking up the phone. Just some terrible things. I had a person mail me a comb once. No note. I used to have very short, very spiky hair. I thought it was quite edgy, and some viewers loved it and some hated it, but I really didn't care because I liked it.

**Jesse Miller:** Do you find that you are capable of separating that professional side where you still have to put a little bit of your personal out there?

**Sophie Lui:** Instagram is different for me. I don't really get as much vitriol on Instagram. It's a happier place than Twitter. I think of Instagram as more my personal life and those parts of me that I'm willing to share. I show pictures of my partner who lives in California and I haven't seen him for months. I'm okay with sharing that. I wouldn't necessarily put that same information on Twitter. On Twitter, you might get an idea of what interests me in society, what articles interest me. You get more of the intellectual side of me on Twitter. On Instagram, you're going to know what I'm having for dinner, or what kind of wine I'm drinking.

**Jesse Miller:** Has Twitter been able to expand your horizons, maybe outside of the everyday of your job?

**Sophie Lui:** As much as Twitter drives me crazy and depresses me sometimes, I haven't quit it and I don't want to, and I still see good in it because I have been able to read... I didn't know who you were until I found you on Twitter. And I find your account very interesting, and your commentary very interesting. So that's one of the pluses for me of Twitter, is finding people like you, finding people like Karen Ho — she asks every day, "Are you doomscrolling?" And I'm like, "Damn it, yes I am again." I have in the past followed some accounts just because I feel like I should, because they're in the public consciousness and I need to know a wide spectrum of things. But that can just eat away at my soul a little bit and so I've stopped that.

**Jesse Miller:** Do you think social media will actually work in your advantage as you take next steps wherever they may take you?

**Sophie Lui:** I think social media will be good for me for a couple of reasons. It'll keep me relevant and in the public eye in a medium that is becoming just as big as the medium that I currently work in. So I think I have to stay on social media if I want to remain relevant. The other and more interesting thing to me is just that I can continue to learn, and that's really what I love about my job anyway — is despite all the negativity, despite the arguing and just the stuff that gets me down sometimes, I really appreciate having this wider intellectual world open to me, that maybe was not as easy to access as efficiently as you can do it now.

## Context

This video features an interview with Sophie Lui, anchor of Global News at 5 and co-anchor of Global News Hour at 6 at Global BC. The interview explores the intersection of social media and traditional journalism, media literacy, and the challenges of being a public figure in the digital age.

Sophie Lui is an award-winning journalist based in Vancouver, BC. She has received multiple awards including a Leo Award for Best Host and a 2023 Canadian Screen Award for Best Local Newscast. She graduated from BCIT's Broadcast Radio program and has worked at CHEK-TV in Victoria and Global BC in Vancouver.
