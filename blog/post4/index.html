In the Public Eye

Being Visible Has a Cost

The Sophie Lui interview this week was one of the more grounding things I have listened to in this course. Lui is a working journalist — six o'clock anchor on Global BC — and her relationship with social media is not theoretical. She describes Twitter as a place where she learns and connects but also a place that "can eat away at my soul" if she follows accounts just because she feels she should. That kind of honesty is rare in conversations about digital identity.

What stood out was her distinction between platforms. Instagram is personal — dinner, wine, her partner. Twitter is where people learn about her personality and her intellectual interests. She is not maintaining separate identities. She is making deliberate choices about what each platform does for her. That is a level of intentionality that most people skip. We tend to use platforms by default rather than by design, and the result is a digital identity that happened to us rather than one we built.

Lui also describes receiving a comb in the mail — no note, no return address — as a response to her short hairstyle on air. That is the cost side of public visibility that is easy to underestimate when you are building a professional presence from behind a screen. The harassment that public figures receive is not a side effect of social media. It is a structural feature of any system that gives anonymous audiences direct access to individuals. Social media did not create misogyny. It gave it a delivery mechanism.

Defining the Term

What Media Literacy Actually Means

Trilling and Fadel (2009) define media literacy as the ability to critically analyze, evaluate, and create media across formats. That definition sounds straightforward until you try to apply it in practice. Media literacy is not just about detecting fake news. It is about understanding how any piece of content was constructed, what choices the creator made, what was included and excluded, and what effect those choices have on how the audience perceives the message.

The MediaSmarts video this week illustrated this well by starting with an AI-generated house hippo video. The question it asks is simple: is this real? But the thinking it requires is not simple at all. It requires understanding how video is produced, what AI generation looks like, why someone might create this, and who benefits from you believing it. That chain of questions is media literacy in action.

Hirst (2019) takes this further by examining the political economy of fake news — the economic incentives that drive misinformation. Fake news is not primarily an information problem. It is a business model. Content designed to provoke outrage generates clicks, which generates ad revenue. Understanding that supply chain is more useful than fact-checking any individual article. Media literacy at its best teaches you to ask not just "is this true?" but "why does this exist? who profits from me sharing it?"

Media literacy gets dismissed for a few reasons. Some people see it as condescending — the implication that they are being fooled. Others see it as politically motivated — an attempt to delegitimize specific media sources. And some people are simply not interested in doing the cognitive work. Doomscrolling is easier than critical analysis. But the cost of low media literacy is not just personal. It is societal. When enough people share misinformation uncritically, the information environment degrades for everyone.

Professional Exposure

Risks & Benefits of Public Engagement

Benefits
  • Reach and visibility for your work and ideas
  • Professional network expansion beyond geography
  • Building credibility through consistent, public engagement
  • Access to diverse perspectives that improve decision-making
  • Recruitment and business development through reputation
Risks
  • Permanent searchability of every public statement
  • Context collapse — content consumed by unintended audiences
  • Harassment and bad-faith engagement
  • Employer liability when professional and personal lines blur
  • Pressure to perform consistency rather than grow authentically

The best approach to negative replies and critiques is the one Lui described: breathe, pause, and decide whether this is someone worth engaging with or someone worth muting. Not every criticism requires a response, and not every response requires a debate. The goal is not to win arguments online. It is to maintain a presence that reflects your values while preserving your energy for the work that actually matters. A PLN that values media literacy is one where the participants have developed the judgment to distinguish between constructive disagreement and performative conflict.
