Inclusion & Community Engagement

Andy HuFebruary 2026Blog Post #3 · Week 4
Communities of Identity

Inclusion Is Not Assimilation

Dr. Shelley Moore makes a distinction that I have been thinking about since watching the video this week. Inclusion is not about making everyone the same. It is about finding the balance between communities of identity - where people share similar backgrounds, interests, and strengths - and communities of diversity, where differences create opportunities to learn from each other (Moore, 2021). The goal is not to suppress what makes someone different in order to fit a group norm. It is to create environments where people can be exactly who they are and still belong.

This maps directly to how I think about building teams. When I am hiring, the instinct is to find people who think the way I think - same tools, same frameworks, same assumptions. That builds a fast, frictionless team. It also builds an echo chamber. The best product decisions I have made came from people who disagreed with me and forced me to reconsider something I was certain about. That kind of productive discomfort is what Moore means by a community of diversity.

Clark and Aufderheide (2009) extend this idea to public media, arguing for platforms that are "open, dynamic, and participatory" rather than top-down broadcast models. The parallel to social media is obvious. The most valuable professional networks are not the ones where everyone agrees - they are the ones where different perspectives collide constructively. Obama's Stanford address reinforced this point: the internet gives everyone a voice, but without media literacy and critical engagement, those voices can fragment into silos of affirmation rather than communities of genuine exchange.

PLN & Diversity

Amplification vs Echo Chambers

Engage Social media enables direct dialogue across geography, industry, and experience level.
Challenge Algorithms optimize for engagement, not learning. They reward polarization over nuance.
Amplify A PLN can make space for voices that mainstream channels overlook - if curated deliberately.

The instructor's video this week asked a pointed question: are you willing to sit in the discomfort of encountering views you disagree with so you can learn why someone believes their version of right is the correct path? That is the real test of a diverse PLN. It is easy to follow people who validate your thinking. It is harder - and more valuable - to follow people who challenge it.

In my work, I have seen this play out in open-source communities. The best projects are not the ones where everyone agrees on the approach. They are the ones with active, sometimes heated, discussions in the issues tab - where contributors from different backgrounds push the codebase in directions the original author never considered. That is inclusion in practice: not suppressing differences, but making them productive.

Professional Context

Employer Policies & Social Media

The question of employer social media policies adds another layer to this conversation. When someone posts something publicly and their employer distances themselves a week later, what does that mean for the employee's freedom of expression? In Canada, the distinction between freedom of expression and the American concept of free speech matters here - expression has limits, and employers have legitimate interests in how their brand is represented.

As someone who runs a company, I think about this from both sides. I want my team to have authentic public presences - it builds credibility for the business. But I also know that one poorly considered post can become a liability. The answer is not to restrict expression but to foster a culture where people understand the difference between having an opinion and having a platform. A PLN that values diversity of thought also needs to value the responsibility that comes with public discourse.

The benefit of a diverse and inclusive PLN in this context is that it provides natural accountability. When your network includes people from different backgrounds and perspectives, you are less likely to post something tone-deaf because you have already been exposed to how that message would land with different audiences. The network itself functions as a kind of editorial check - not through censorship, but through exposure. The more perspectives you engage with, the better your judgment becomes about what is worth saying publicly and how to say it well.
