## Chapter 6: Developing Personal Learning Networks for Open and Social Learning

*Extracted from: Veletsianos, G. (Ed.). (2010). Emerging Technologies in Distance Education. Athabasca University Press. Open Access.*

### Theoretical Foundations

Several overlapping bodies of theory and practice informed the development and facilitation of EC&I 831 (a graduate-level open course). The chapter draws from: the open movement, complementary learning theories, and connectivism.

**The Open Movement:** An informal, worldwide phenomenon characterized by the tendency of individuals and groups to work, collaborate and publish in ways that favour accessibility, sharing, transparency and interoperability. Advocates of openness value the democratization of knowledge construction and dissemination, and are critical of knowledge controlling structures (Couros, 2006).

**Complementary Learning Theories:**
- **Social Cognitive Theory (SCT):** Humans learn through observations of other individuals. Self-efficacy beliefs are the most influential arbiter of human activity.
- **Social Constructivism (Vygotsky):** Knowledge is socially constructed through interaction with others. The Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) describes learning potential enhanced through collaboration.
- **Andragogy (Knowles):** Adult learners are self-directed, draw on experience, have intrinsic motivation, and value problem-centered learning.

**Connectivism (Siemens, 2005):** A learning theory for the digital age. Key principles include:
- Learning and knowledge rests in diversity of opinions
- Learning is a process of connecting specialized nodes or information sources
- The capacity to know more is more critical than what is currently known
- Nurturing and maintaining connections is needed to facilitate continual learning
- Decision-making itself is a learning process

### A Model of Open Teaching

Couros describes an "emerging model of open teaching" characterized by:
1. **Content is freely and openly shared** (OER, Creative Commons licensing)
2. **Course participation is open** to both registered and non-registered students
3. **Course activities are designed for public sharing** (blogs, wikis, social media)
4. **Assessment is transparent** and aligned with open practices
5. **Students develop Personal Learning Networks** as a core course outcome

### EC&I 831: The Open Course

EC&I 831 was a graduate-level course at the University of Regina that served as the primary case study. The course was designed to be "open" -- allowing anyone on the internet to participate alongside registered students. Key features included:
- Use of blogs as primary communication and reflection tools
- Twitter for synchronous discussion and network building
- Wiki for collaborative knowledge construction
- Elluminate (synchronous sessions) with guest speakers
- Diigo for social bookmarking
- All course content openly available

### Developing Personal Learning Networks

The chapter argues that the development of a PLN is both a process and an outcome of open teaching. Key insights include:

**PLN as Process:** Students are expected to actively build their networks throughout the course by engaging with experts, peers, and open communities. This involves following relevant professionals on social media, subscribing to blogs and feeds, participating in online discussions, and attending virtual events.

**PLN as Outcome:** By the end of the course, students have developed a functional PLN that extends beyond the course itself. This network becomes a lifelong resource for professional learning and development.

**Network building strategies:**
- Identifying and following key thinkers in one's field
- Actively contributing to online communities
- Sharing resources and ideas openly
- Engaging in reciprocal interactions
- Using multiple platforms to diversify network connections

### Personal Learning Environments (PLE) vs. Personal Learning Networks (PLN)

The chapter distinguishes between PLEs and PLNs:
- **PLE:** The tools, artefacts, processes, and physical connections that allow learners to control and manage their learning
- **PLN:** The people and communities within one's network that provide learning opportunities and support

The PLN is understood as the human/social dimension of the broader PLE.

### Final Thoughts

Couros reflects on common questions: "How did you get away with this?" and concerns about the risks of open teaching. He argues that while open teaching requires courage and institutional support, the benefits -- deeper learning, authentic audience, professional network development -- far outweigh the risks.

The chapter concludes that Personal Learning Networks represent a fundamental shift in how we think about professional development and lifelong learning. Rather than relying solely on formal educational institutions, learners can now build robust, self-directed networks that support ongoing learning throughout their careers.
