The V&R Framework

White and Le Cornu (2017) offer a more useful model than the "digital native/immigrant" binary that has dominated the conversation for years. Instead of categorizing people by age or comfort level, they look at modes of engagement. A Visitor goes online with a specific task, completes it, and leaves without a lasting social trace. A Resident builds a persistent presence, contributes to communities, and leaves identity artifacts behind.

Most people are not purely one or the other. They are visitors on some platforms and residents on others, and the same person might be a resident professionally (active on LinkedIn, contributing to industry forums) while being a visitor personally (browsing recipes, checking weather). The two axes (Visitor/Resident and Personal/Professional) create a map that is far more descriptive than any generational label.

The most interesting thing this map reveals is where the overlap happens. Twitter sits right in the middle. It started as a personal feed and gradually became a professional tool for sharing work, engaging with other developers, and following industry conversations. That drift from personal to professional happened without me planning it, which is exactly the kind of convergence that the V&R framework is designed to surface.

GitHub is my most resident-professional platform. Every commit is a public artifact. Stack Overflow contributions persist indefinitely. These are platforms where the work itself is the identity, and there is no profile photo or bio that matters more than the quality of what you ship. On the personal side, Discord and Instagram are where I am most resident, but those communities are bounded. They do not bleed into professional contexts the way Twitter does.

Privacy & Reputation

The Employer Question

The course asks how an employer would respond if they assessed your social media presence in real time during an interview. As someone who is both an employer and a candidate in different contexts, I can speak to both sides. When I am evaluating someone to work with, whether a contractor, a hire, or a partner, I absolutely look them up. I am not looking for controversy. I am looking for signal. Do they ship work? Do they engage thoughtfully in public discussions? Do they demonstrate the kind of thinking I want on my team?

The reverse is also true. Clients look me up. Investors look me up. Partners look me up. My digital presence is doing a job interview 24 hours a day whether I am aware of it or not. That is not paranoia. It is just how professional evaluation works now. The question is not whether to have a digital presence, but whether the one you have is working for you or against you.

Data privacy both limits and promotes a PLN. On the limiting side, every platform you join is a data relationship you are entering. Your professional network on LinkedIn is also LinkedIn's dataset about your industry, your connections, and your career trajectory. On the promoting side, the willingness to be visible, to have a searchable, attributable presence, is what makes a PLN functional. You cannot build a professional network while remaining invisible. The tension between privacy and visibility is not a problem to solve. It is a trade-off to manage deliberately.
