## Transcript

*Press conference at the 49th annual meeting of the World Economic Forum, Davos. Panel includes Manju George (WEF), Prime Minister Yuri Ratas (Estonia), James Manyika (McKinsey Global Institute), Nandan Nilekani (Infosys/Aadhaar, India), Mary Snapp (Microsoft Philanthropies), and Mike Kubzansky (Omidyar Network).*

**Moderator:** Ladies and gentlemen, good afternoon and welcome to this press conference from Davos. You're watching this press conference at the 49th annual meeting of the World Economic Forum. You're joining a press conference titled "The Value of Digital Identity for the Global Economy and Society."

**Manju George (WEF):** Over 60% of global GDP is expected to be digitized by 2022. More and more people, businesses, devices, and things are interacting online, and digital identity sits at the heart of those interactions. How we construct and use digital identities determines how people are represented online, what opportunities they have access to, and who is trusted online. So it's quite a foundational role that identity plays in digital economies and societies.

There are a couple of areas that need our attention. One is to make sure that the opportunities associated with digital identity are distributed widely. Today only about 50% of the world's population is using the internet. According to the World Bank, over a billion people do not have any form of legally recognized identity -- that's excluding them from participating in the digital economy. There are divides: 60% of those people live in lower middle-income countries, 50% are in sub-Saharan Africa. In the lower income countries surveyed, about 40% of women don't have access to identity compared to about 30% of men. If we don't prioritize inclusion, we risk exacerbating some of the divides that already exist.

The second priority is the quality of the identity. Does the digital identity give you access to a range of meaningful opportunities in your daily life? Does it keep me and my data secure? Does it protect me against discrimination, exclusion, and harms? Does it give me control over how my identity and related data is used? There are tricky questions: how do you balance an individual's right to privacy and a nation's need to protect its citizens?

**Prime Minister Yuri Ratas (Estonia):** Digital identity is one of the cornerstones of the digital economy. Every digital service that has legal consequence or business value needs a secure digital identity. In Estonia, we decided to solve this challenge already 20 years ago through a shared, uniform national identity platform, built not just for all public services but also for the whole economy.

Since the ID card is mandatory in Estonia, it gave many companies an automatic customer base, making it profitable to build new services on it. Digital identities can support new business generation and innovation. In Estonia, digital IDs are used for doing taxes, e-voting, e-prescription, e-banking, e-school, and most importantly for signing any documents digitally. Digital signing alone saves every working Estonian at least an estimated five business days per year, amounting to a total efficiency gain of 2% of GDP annually. As they say, time is money.

In 2014, we started issuing our digital identity as e-residency to people from all corners of the world. There are almost 50,000 e-residents from 168 different countries. We are growing as an economy faster than we otherwise would because we have embraced digital business space, and strong identity has been key to making it possible.

**James Manyika (McKinsey Global Institute):** We've just completed an extensive study of the issue of digital identity. We looked deeply at about seven countries: Brazil, China, Ethiopia, India, Nigeria, the US, and the UK -- a very diverse set of countries with quite different challenges when it comes to identification and being able to fully participate.

When we say fully participate, we're thinking about people in several roles: people as consumers, people as workers where it's quite extraordinary how often the issue of identification comes up when trying to get employment, people as micro entrepreneurs trying to set up businesses, people as citizens trying to get access to government services, and people who have rights and assets and ownership. Quite often women suffer the most on this issue.

We examined something like over a hundred use cases and found that the economic impact is actually quite substantial. The range of impacts ranges from about 3% to as much as 13% of GDP of these countries, especially in developing economies. Half of the economic impact accrues to the users themselves through participation, financial inclusion. The World Bank has denominated that something like 1.7 billion people could be more financially included if they had access to digital IDs. Impact also goes to the institutions involved because they save money on fraud and risk.

Full implementation requires that we think about what a good ID looks like and how we mitigate some of the risks, because digital identifications are often what you might call dual-use technologies that could be quite easy to misuse.

**Nandan Nilekani (India/Aadhaar):** India started its digital ID journey exactly 10 years back because of the need to make its delivery of public services more efficient -- the lack of IDs was leading to a lot of fraud. It was also an inclusion measure because many people didn't have birth certificates or any form of ID and needed some ID to migrate, to travel, to get a job.

I was asked by the then Indian government to join. I joined in July of 2009 and worked in the government for about four and a half years, building a very modern digital ID platform using biometric deduplication. When I stepped down in March of 2014, there were about 600 million people on the platform. Today there's about 1.2 billion people on the platform. It has been very successful under two different governments.

People use it to open a bank account, get a mobile connection, for identity when they go to the plane. It's also linked to the financial system -- about 600 million people have IDs linked to bank accounts. In the last five years, 300 million new people have opened bank accounts using things like the ID. India today runs the world's largest direct cash transfer program running into billions of dollars into a few hundred million people's bank accounts, and it's all electronic.

We had a lot of concerns about privacy and security. The matter went up to the Indian Supreme Court. The Indian Supreme Court held that Indians do have a fundamental right to privacy but it could be circumscribed under certain circumstances, and then they upheld that Aadhaar meets that privacy requirement. The law that governs the ID as well as the Supreme Court judgment makes it clear that it cannot be used for any other purpose -- so a lot of purpose limits have been put on the ID.

In developing countries, it's a leapfrog. In Western countries, people have IDs and need a digital ID. In developing countries, people have no ID and we're using this technology opportunity to give everybody a digital ID.

**Mary Snapp (Microsoft Philanthropies):** Perhaps the thing I would focus on is thinking about the importance and the need for digital identity for people who are on the move -- the 75 million or so people on the move today, that number will continue to grow. For many of these people whose identity is captured by a piece of paper that is easily lost or stolen, they then become in essence stateless. The ability to receive any kind of services related to education, health, or registration is severely hampered.

We along with a number of others were founding members of ID2020. This organization has come together to create a coalition of founding members to create a platform by which digital identity can be created that could be used by others -- not one particular use of technology, but a set of standards by which digital identity could be developed in many different use cases using many different kinds of platforms and applications. For us, thinking about the role that companies can play in helping to submit technology to create the standards is really critical.

As we go into areas like artificial intelligence and enhanced reality, we know that there are ethical standards that we have learned from our work in privacy that need to be adapted and even taken further. As the technology is developed, we need some ethicists, philosophers, and historians alongside us to ensure ethical use.

**Mike Kubzansky (Omidyar Network):** We talk a lot about not just any ID but what we call "good ID" -- an ID that could be both government-issued or comes from your virtual digital trail or combination of that identity. An ID that really fundamentally empowers and protects the individual and provides agency and control for the individual over the use of that identity.

If there is no trust, that benefit cannot really be realized. Where does trust come from? It comes from control, privacy, agency, and confidence that the data and the identity you've signed up for is not going to be exploited. We've invested in companies like Digi.me, which allow you to control and manage the flow of data and decide who you want to share it with, and Learning Machine, which does work with credentials that you can then permission out.

**Q&A Discussion on balancing security, privacy, and governance:**

James Manyika noted that the balance between trust and security is a question for all countries, not just specific ones. Every country needs to navigate this thoughtfully with considerations of ethics, privacy, and individual sense of agency. Nandan Nilekani shared that India's Aadhaar case was the second longest running case in the Indian Supreme Court, with all issues of privacy, data breaches, security, and surveillance discussed in great detail. Prime Minister Ratas emphasized that in Estonia, the principle is that the person who gives their data owns their data, and they can always check who has been accessing their data.

On governance frameworks, Manyika advocated for agnosticism about who implements the systems, but stressed the need for accountability mechanisms that allow transparency, review, and auditing. Snapp emphasized separating the question of data from governance, arguing that no single entity should manage and control the breadth of data across public and private sectors.
